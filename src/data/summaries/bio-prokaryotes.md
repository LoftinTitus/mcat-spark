# Prokaryotes

## Overview
Prokaryotes are unicellular organisms that lack a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles. They include Bacteria and Archaea, and are among the most diverse and abundant life forms on Earth. Their unique features are highly relevant for the MCAT.

---

## 1. Classification
- **Domains:** Bacteria and Archaea
- **Shapes:** Cocci (spherical), bacilli (rod-shaped), spirilla (spiral)
- **Arrangements:** Single, chains (strepto-), clusters (staphylo-)

---

## 2. Cell Structure
- **Nucleoid:** Region containing circular DNA (no nuclear membrane)
- **Plasmids:** Small, circular DNA molecules; often carry antibiotic resistance genes
- **Cell wall:**
  - **Bacteria:** Peptidoglycan (Gram-positive: thick, Gram-negative: thin + outer membrane)
  - **Archaea:** Pseudopeptidoglycan or other polymers
- **Capsule:** Polysaccharide layer for protection, adhesion, immune evasion
- **Flagella:** For motility (rotary motion)
- **Pili/fimbriae:** Attachment, conjugation (sex pilus)
- **Ribosomes:** 70S (target of many antibiotics)
- **No membrane-bound organelles**

---

## 3. Metabolism & Growth
- **Energy sources:**
  - **Phototrophs:** Use light (e.g., cyanobacteria)
  - **Chemotrophs:** Use chemicals (organic/inorganic)
- **Oxygen requirements:**
  - **Obligate aerobes:** Require O₂
  - **Obligate anaerobes:** Killed by O₂
  - **Facultative anaerobes:** Can use O₂ or not
  - **Aerotolerant anaerobes:** Ignore O₂
- **Nitrogen fixation:** Some convert N₂ to ammonia (e.g., Rhizobium)
- **Binary fission:** Asexual reproduction
- **Endospores:** Dormant, resistant structures (e.g., Bacillus, Clostridium)

---

## 4. Genetic Exchange
- **Transformation:** Uptake of naked DNA from environment
- **Transduction:** Gene transfer via bacteriophages
- **Conjugation:** Direct transfer via sex pilus (F plasmid)
- **Horizontal gene transfer:** Major driver of diversity and antibiotic resistance

---

## 5. Bacteria vs. Archaea
- **Bacteria:** Peptidoglycan cell wall, ester-linked membrane lipids, sensitive to antibiotics
- **Archaea:** No peptidoglycan, ether-linked lipids, extremophiles, not sensitive to most antibiotics

---

## 6. Clinical & MCAT Relevance
- **Pathogens:** Many human diseases (e.g., TB, strep throat, E. coli infections)
- **Normal flora:** Beneficial bacteria (gut microbiome)
- **Antibiotics:** Target cell wall, ribosomes, DNA gyrase
- **Antibiotic resistance:** Plasmids, horizontal gene transfer
- **Biotechnology:** Recombinant DNA, CRISPR, industrial fermentation

---

## Quiz

**Q1: What is the main structural difference between Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria?**
A) Presence of a nucleus
B) Thickness of the peptidoglycan cell wall *[CORRECT]
C) Presence of mitochondria
D) Type of ribosome
**Explanation:** Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan wall; Gram-negative have a thin wall plus an outer membrane.

**Q2: Which process allows bacteria to take up DNA from their environment?**
A) Conjugation
B) Transformation *[CORRECT]
C) Transduction
D) Binary fission
**Explanation:** Transformation is the uptake of naked DNA from the environment.

**Q3: What is the function of bacterial endospores?**
A) Energy production
B) Motility
C) Survival under harsh conditions *[CORRECT]
D) Protein synthesis
**Explanation:** Endospores allow bacteria to survive extreme conditions.

**Q4: Which of the following is unique to Archaea compared to Bacteria?**
A) Peptidoglycan cell wall
B) Ether-linked membrane lipids *[CORRECT]
C) 70S ribosomes
D) Binary fission
**Explanation:** Archaea have ether-linked membrane lipids; bacteria have ester-linked.

**Q5: What is the primary mechanism of antibiotic resistance spread among bacteria?**
A) Mutation only
B) Horizontal gene transfer *[CORRECT]
C) Endospore formation
D) Binary fission
**Explanation:** Horizontal gene transfer (conjugation, transformation, transduction) spreads resistance genes rapidly.
